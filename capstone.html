Capstone Project: ENV-ISION

ENV-ISION is an autonomous boat trailering system designed to streamline the docking process. With a press of a button, the boat moves into position, drives onto a trailer, and securely latches—all without human intervention. Built with the support of ENVGO, this project prioritizes convenience, safety, and autonomy.

Team & Concept

  • Designed by 5 Mechatronics students (Mechanical, Electrical, Embedded, Controls)
  • Single-person autonomous trailering (vs typical 2-person manual process)
  • Camera detects trailer target → computes pose → feeds controller to drive thrusters

Breakdown by Discipline

🛠️ Mechanical

Designed the waterproof chassis, latch, and thruster layout. CAD in SolidWorks, fabricated in acrylic & aluminum.

🔌 Electrical

Managed LiPo battery system, ESC setup, fusing, and waterproof electronics integration. Designed wiring schematics and distribution boards.

💻 Embedded Systems (My Focus)

  • Established fast, reliable UART communication between Raspberry Pi and Arduino microcontrollers.
  • Processed AprilTag vision data using OpenCV, with camera calibration and pose frame transformations.
  • Designed a robust data packet protocol for motor PWM signals, including delimiter-based error handling and handshake synchronization.
  • Optimized camera processing by disabling live video feed and tuning resolution for real-time performance.
  • Implemented Butterworth filters to reduce pose data noise and stabilize motor control.
  • Tuned PID controllers for heading (θ) and forward movement (y-axis) using filtered vision data.
  • Tested and debugged embedded systems under real-world disturbances (wind, water currents).

🧠 Controls

Implemented a dynamic feed-forward and PID-based control system for precise autonomous docking.

  • Created a dynamic trajectory planner to generate time-dependent position targets for x, y, and θ (heading) during trailering.
  • Designed a feed-forward controller to compute required accelerations based on target trajectories and boat dynamics.
  • Implemented 3 PID controllers (x, y, θ) tuned independently to minimize steady-state error and stabilize approach under real-world disturbances.
  • Applied Butterworth low-pass filters to AprilTag vision data to remove high-frequency noise while preserving critical motion signals.
  • Prioritized tuning for θ (heading) first, then y (forward), then x (lateral), ensuring smooth correction without oscillations.
  • Validated final control performance achieving ±0 cm error in y-position, ±5 cm error in x-position, and ±3° orientation error during testing.
